What the evaluation metrics mean
The 6 Turnitin mechanisms
M1 — Transformer model proxy (bino score)
Turnitin uses a fine-tuned AI model to decide whether each sentence reads as human or machine-generated. We approximate this using the bino score: the ratio of how surprising each word is under a precise model versus a loose one. The more predictable the word choices, the more negative the score. Threshold: scores below −4.45 are flagged as AI. Human writing typically sits between −4.2 and −3.5.
M2 — Overlapping window analysis
Turnitin doesn't just score sentences individually — it scores them in overlapping groups of 6 (sliding windows). A window is flagged when its average bino score drops below −4.45. This catches AI text that is embedded within otherwise human text. Even if no single sentence fails, a cluster of borderline sentences in a row will flag a window.
M3 — Sentence aggregation (consecutive runs)
When 4 or more sentences in a row all score below −4.35, Turnitin's aggregation mechanism activates. This is the most reliable way it detects AI writing — not one bad sentence, but a sustained stretch of uniformly predictable text. A run of 4+ is the single highest-risk pattern to have in your essay.
M4 — Long-form prose requirement
Turnitin only scores qualifying long-form prose: full sentences inside paragraphs. It ignores bullet points, reference lists, headers, and short fragments. This tool does the same — only sentences of 5+ words inside paragraphs are included in the analysis.
M5 — Paraphrase / spinner detection
Turnitin also flags text that was AI-generated and then paraphrased through a spinning tool. The fingerprint is a preserved grammatical skeleton (high function-word ratio) combined with unusual content words swapped in. This tool checks for this pattern and flags any sentences that match it.
M6 — Multilingual model
Turnitin detects the language and routes text to a language-specific model. For English academic prose it uses the English model. British spelling matters: "organisation" not "organization", "centred" not "centered". American spellings in British-English assignments are themselves a signal. This tool checks for em dashes, which are one of the strongest individual AI signals regardless of language.
The 5 linguistic families
Family 1 — Surface & stylometric
Low-level writing patterns: how many unique words you use (MATTR), how often you use "I", how much sentence length varies (CV), punctuation density, reading level. AI writing tends to have uniform sentence lengths, low first-person usage, and slightly elevated vocabulary complexity. Em dashes are the single most reliable surface signal — remove every one.
Family 2 — Discourse & pragmatic
How claims are framed and how the text guides the reader. Hedges ("tends to", "often", "may") signal a human acknowledging uncertainty. Boosters ("clearly", "certainly", "crucial") are overused by AI. Stance markers ("I think", "In my view") show personal judgement. AI writing typically lacks these or uses them formulaically.
Family 3 — Epistemic & content
Whether the text sounds grounded in genuine knowledge and experience. Named entities (real people, places, institutions) signal that a human who knows the subject wrote this. Personal experience markers ("I have come across", "what I kept noticing") are very difficult for AI to fake convincingly. Citations show engagement with actual sources.
Family 4 — Predictability & probabilistic
Perplexity measures how surprised a language model is by each sentence — high perplexity means the words are hard to predict, which is a human signal. Perplexity stdev measures whether the essay has varied difficulty: human writing naturally spikes between simple and complex sentences. Top-1 bigram rate measures how often each word is the single most likely next word — high rates indicate formulaic phrasing.
Family 5 — Provenance
Origin verification — whether the text carries a watermark or fingerprint left by the generator. This tool cannot check Turnitin's watermark database, but it does check for paraphrase/spinner fingerprints (M5 above). Essays written and edited manually have no generator signature.
IMPORTANT — self-referential scoring note
This tool builds its language model from the essay you paste. This means sentences score better against it than they would against Turnitin's external model trained on millions of documents. If this tool flags something, Turnitin almost certainly flags it too. Use the results as a minimum baseline — the real score is likely slightly higher.
